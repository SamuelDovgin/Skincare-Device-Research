🔥 SHR & Multi-Flash Thermal Simulator

Set the parameters and watch how the epidermis, hair follicle, and the deep stem-cell target heat up over time — and whether successive flashes (Ulike-style "SHR" / stacked pulses) actually push past the threshold for follicle damage. Timing is the hero variable.

What this shows — and what it can't

The question behind Ulike's "SHR mode" and every "8.33 J/cm² over 3 flashes" claim: do successive flashes add up as heat at the follicle, and does that heat reach a damaging threshold?

A single flash heats the pigmented hair shaft/bulb fast, then it cools. Fire again before it cools and the heat stacks higher; fire again after it cools and you've just re-dosed a weaker shot. Meanwhile the epidermis (which you must protect) and the deep, unpigmented bulge stem cells (the target for permanent reduction, reached only by slow heat diffusion) each run on their own clock. This tool integrates a simple lumped-parameter thermal model of all three so you can see the interplay.

Read this as a teaching model, not a lab measurement. It uses published thermal-relaxation and thermal-damage-time values (cited below) to reproduce the qualitative physics — the shapes, the timing thresholds, the tradeoffs. The absolute temperatures and the "damage threshold" are illustrative and deliberately conservative; real follicle heating depends on melanin distribution, blood perfusion, spot size and optical scattering that a lumped model can't resolve. Use it to build intuition about timing and stacking, not to predict a number for your skin. Not medical advice.

Timing & pulse count — the part that decides stacking

Edge-to-edge delay. Under the follicle's ~10–100 ms cooling time → heat stacks. Over it → each flash resets. Ulike SHR ≈ 250 ms (4/s); pro SHR ≈ 100 ms (10 Hz); clinical fixed-spot stacking ≈ 10–40 ms. Not disclosed by any home device — an honest unknown.
One strong shot, or many weak ones (an SHR / in-motion pass hits a spot repeatedly).
Drives skin safety directly. For the same J/cm², a shorter pulse spikes the epidermis hotter (less time to shed heat); a longer one is gentler on skin but bleeds peak follicle temperature. FDA-disclosed widths run ~0.4–12 ms.

Skin, hair & options

Darker skin = more epidermal melanin = the epidermis absorbs more and heats toward the burn zone.
Coarse, dark hair holds more melanin (heats harder) and cools slower (longer follicle TRT).
Actively pulls heat out of the epidermis and lowers its floor — lets you push fluence without burning.
The fair test: spend all your Joules in one strong flash, or split them across many? (dashed gray = the single-flash follicle curve).

3 · The core tradeoff — one strong flash vs. many weak ones

Same Joules, split two ways. This is the actual decision behind "SHR mode" and split-pulse settings.

⚡ One strong flash — the "peak" pathway

All the energy arrives at once, so the follicle spikes to a high peak temperature (often >65 °C) and coagulates protein immediately. This is classic selective photothermolysis. It's efficient at killing the follicle in one shot — but that same peak lands on the epidermis too, so it hurts more and needs strong cooling. Best when hair is dark/coarse and the device can deliver real single-flash fluence (5–6+ J/cm²).

🔁 Many weak flashes — the "accumulation" pathway

Each flash is gentle (low peak, comfortable), but if they arrive fast enough relative to the deep target's slow cooling, the stem-cell dose builds up across the sequence — the "gradual bulk heating to ~45–48 °C" that SHR markets. It's gentler on skin and great for pain/comfort. The catch: it only works if the flashes are frequent enough and there are enough of them. Split the energy too thin, too slow, or too few times and neither pathway reaches threshold.

4 · The Ulike case — what "SHR mode" and "4 flashes" actually mean

Ulike (Air 10 / Air 3 / X) markets an "SHR mode." Here's the honest read on what it delivers, mapped onto the physics above.

Air 10 burst: up to 4 flashes (2 lamps ×2) Burst energy ≈26 J Effective ≈6.67 J/cm² for the whole burst ≈1.7 J/cm² per sub-flash SHR cadence ≈4 flashes/s (≈250 ms apart) AutoGlide ≈2 flashes/s (500 ms) Sapphire cooling ≈20 °C

Two things fall straight out of the model when you load the Ulike Air 10 SHR preset:

  1. The ~250 ms cadence is far slower than the follicle's ~10–100 ms cooling time, so successive flashes do not stack into a higher follicle peak — the peak stays modest and comfortable (that's the whole point of "nearly painless"). Ulike's SHR is not the fast 10–40 ms fixed-spot stacking that raises peak temperature.
  2. Its bet is the accumulation pathway. 250 ms is on the same order as the deep target's slow thermal-damage-time window (~170–1000 ms), so if a spot gets hit enough times (an in-motion pass, not one press), the bulge dose can creep up. Whether it clears threshold depends on total flash count, per-flash fluence, and hair darkness — exactly the sliders above. On fine or light hair, the model shows it struggling to get there.

This mirrors the write-up's conclusion for the Fansizhe "triple-pulse" mode: an "accumulated fluence" headline (26 J, 6.67 J/cm²) is only meaningful if the timing supports stacking, and the honest lever remains the highest comfortable single-flash fluence plus finishing the full course. SHR's real, evidence-backed advantage is comfort/safety at equal efficacy — see the trials in §6. Full multi-flash write-up →

5 · Why timing rules everything — the two clocks

Every threshold in this tool comes from these published timescales. Heat "sticks" only when energy arrives faster than the target sheds it.

StructureTimescaleWhat it means for flash timing
Epidermis (TRT)~3–10 ms23Sheds heat fast → the basis for pulse-splitting & contact cooling. Keep gaps > this so skin recovers.
Hair follicle / shaft (TRT)~10–100 ms (terminal ≈100)456The stacking threshold. Gaps under this → flashes build a higher peak. Gaps over it → no peak gain.
Stem-cell target — Thermal Damage Time~170–1000 ms (optimal ≈400)789The accumulation window. Heat must be sustained this long to diffuse to the unpigmented target. This is why "many pulses over ~1 s" (SHR) can work.
Epidermal safety ceiling< ~65 °C3Above this → burns / pigment change. The reason not to over-stack or over-fluence, especially on darker skin.

Two named mechanisms describe the accumulation pathway. Progressive photothermolysis: "Repeated and fast emission of pulses of low energy progressively heats the chromophore to temperatures of 45–50° over a period of time and safeguards the epidermis from overheating."10 And the extended theory of selective photothermolysis (Altshuler 2001), which introduced Thermal Damage Time — the time for the whole target, heater plus the non-absorbing structure it must warm by diffusion, to reach damaging temperature.7

6 · The clinical evidence on SHR / low-fluence high-rep hair removal

SHR is the one multi-pulse technique with real hair-removal trial data. The pattern: usually comparable to single-pass high fluence, occasionally better, reliably less painful — and it works by a many-pulses-in-motion delivery, not 2–4 shots at a fixed spot.

StudyComparisonResult
Braun 200911SHR (5–10 J/cm², 10 Hz) vs. single-pass (25–40 J/cm², 1 Hz)Comparable 86–91% reduction; SHR much less painful
Omi 201712Dynamic 10 Hz/10 J/cm² vs. static 1 Hz/30 J/cm²No significant difference (41.4%, both); long-term effect attributed to apoptosis, not just bulk heating
Koo 201413Soprano SHR in-motion vs. LightSheer single-pass40.7% vs. 33.5% (n.s.); SHR significantly less pain
Li 201614Same laser, SHR vs. high-fluence modeSHR slightly better (90.2% vs. 87%) and far less painful (VAS 2.75 vs. 6.75)
Wanitphakdeedecha 201215Diode SHR vs. Nd:YAG single-pass high-fluenceNd:YAG won (54.2% vs. 35.7%) — SHR isn't universally superior
Barolet 201216Low-fluence (15 J/cm²) alone at 5 HzSignificant permanent reduction after 4 monthly sessions

Note the fluences: clinical SHR uses 5–15 J/cm² per pulse and dozens of pulses per pass. A home device at ~1.7 J/cm²/sub-flash is well below that per-pulse — which is why the model treats home "SHR" as a comfort-forward, accumulation-dependent mode whose ceiling is set by how many times a spot is actually hit.

7 · The lever that most reliably matters: dose

Across the cleanest home-device data, higher single-flash fluence buys more durable reduction. Splitting energy thinner rarely beats spending it as fluence.

Fluence12-month hair reductionSource

Takeaway for a home user: you can't change your device's J/cm², so maximize safe dose — highest comfortable single-flash energy, contact cooling on, and complete the full course. Multi-flash "SHR" is a comfort tool, not a shortcut to more dose. And beware the flip side: heat below threshold on hormonal areas (face/neck) can provoke paradoxical regrowth rather than reduction.
